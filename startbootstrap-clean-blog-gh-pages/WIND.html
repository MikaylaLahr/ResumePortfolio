Analysis of Offshore Wind Potential

Spring 2021

Overview

For my team's final project in Future Energy System, we evaluated whether off-shore wind turbines are the most viable renewable energy source for the tri-state are (New York, Connecticut and New Jersey). The coastline data maintained by the Office of Ocean and Coastal Resource Management of the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) shows New York with 2625 miles, New Jersey with 1792 miles, and Connecticut with 618 miles of shoreline. Considering the extensive coastline in the tri-state area, offshore wind turbines could potentially provide the area with clean and reliable electricity. This research explored the techniques for using publicly available resources to calculate offshore wind power capabilities. Using wind speed data from meteorological buoys, the annual energy output of four representative offshore wind turbines were calculated, and the maximum number of turbines was based on the area available determined by the wind regime, wake effects, and turbine footprint.

Toward this end, the group conducted research on the following questions: What percentage of the electricity consumed by the tri-state area could offshore wind turbines provide? More specifically, can offshore wind turbines provide all the electricity needed for residential use and how does that compare with the electricity demand of commercial use? Is offshore wind the most feasible renewable energy source for that area, or would land wind farms, solar farms or a combination provide a better alternative?

Off-Shore Wind Turbine Modeling, Buoy Datasets, and Analysis

To address these questions, the team used buoy datasets SDHN4, 44065, 44039, and 44009 to determine the amount of energy that can be provided by wind turbines in each of those areas. Turbine modeling was based on cut-in and cut-out wind speeds of 4 m/s and 25-m/s wind speeds respectively, which are typical for most modern turbines. An energy analysis was performed using turbine models with rotor diameter of 164 m, swept area of 21124 m2, and hub-heights of 100 m achieving nominal rated power of 8-MW at 13 m/s. I worked with another team member, to utilize the programming platform MATLAB for determining the Average Hourly Wind Speed, Hub Height Wind Speed, Power Output per Hour, Monthly Output, using the datasets from each of the four buoys. The Power Output per Hour and Monthly Output of the turbine models along with the maximum number possible at each buoy location were compared to actual monthly demand. The team used public documents from the U.S. Department of Energy, spanning January 2001 through January 2021, to determine total monthly electricity consumption for each state, categorized according to residential, commercial, industrial, transportation, and other uses.

Conclusion

The capacity factor values for the wind turbine near each of four buoys locations were found to be less than other types of energy technologies such as natural gas, coal and nuclear. However, the environmental benefits of wind resources as input energy for offshore wind turbines is an important consideration. In the end, offshore wind farms were not able to provide enough power to New York and New Jersey to be considered as a primary source of energy. Nevertheless, since Connecticut uses less electricity than New York and New Jersey, an offshore wind farm could have a big impact on the residential sector. In order to counteract the lower wind speeds during the warmer months, it is best to combine multiple sources of renewable energy. Solar energy is the most likely and efficient renewable energy source for this application.
