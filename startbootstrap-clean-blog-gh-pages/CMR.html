Overview

Cornell Mars Rover (CMR) is a student-run engineering project team with the goal of competing at the Annual University Rover Challenge at the Mars Desert Research Center in Utah.

Drives Subsystems

Frame and ECore

The frame of the rover was responsible for providing structure to act as the central body of the rover. Many other subsystems, both within and outside of Drives, need to integrate with the frame. This requireed the collaboration of several different members across different subteams. The Fall 2022 frame design consists of a combination between a planar frame and a box frame. This design sought to reduce weight while, using the planar frame, while also maximizing the mounting space for the camera mast and Astrotech systems in the back of the rover on the box frame. The frame was made from aluminum 6061 and carbon fiber connection bars. The majority of the bars used in the design were aluminum 6061. The carbon fiber bars were located at the front of the frame, connected to the arm mount to provide the structural integrity needed for the weight of the arm.

The ECore was responsible for housing all of the needed electrical equipment and boards for the rover. Requirements included that ECore needed to have sufficient air cooling in order for the electrical boards to not overheat. The top cover of the ECore also needed to be secure when closed, but also provide accessibility when placing or altering electrical boards. The main design constraints surrounding the Ecore included the shape and dimensions of the frame and the size, number, and placement of the electrical boards.

Suspension

The suspension of the rover supported the rover by connecting the wheels to the frame. The goal of the suspension system was to mitigate the forces and impulses transmitted to the frame, provide a smooth method of traversal over rough terrain, and mitigate the chances of flipping. These goals were accomplished through the use of a rocker-bogie suspension system. The rocker-bogie suspension system pivots around two joints. The rocker joint is connected to the frame as well as the rod going to the differential bar. When the rocker mount rotates the rod is either pushed forward or backwards, resulting the opposite motion on the other side of the suspension. The bogie joint connects the front rocker arm to the bogie that rotates around the connection point. The rocker-bogie suspension allows for all six wheels to remain in contact with the ground throughout traversal. This feature helps with the stability of the rover and prevents tipping.

The suspension design for Fall 2022 was made from aluminum 6061 connection bars to reduce the cost of the rover. The connection mounts for the rocker and bogie joints will be CNC'd from aluminum 6061 to maintain structural integrity. The other connectors will be printed from carbon fiber filament on the Onyx printers at the RPL. The wheel mounts will be CNC'd from aluminum 6061. The same differential bar that is on the Spring 2021 rover will be used in this design.

Wheels

The wheels of the Cornell Mars rover interfaced with the rocker-bogie suspension system. They were connected to the suspension through the wheel mount interface. The six wheels on the rover were needed for several different reasons:

  • Provide an interface between the terrain and the rover.
  • Attach to the wheel mounts and rotate by way of the electrical system.
  • Mitigate the forces on the suspension and thus the frame of the rover.

    • The wheels were powered by Maxon motors, with the goal of using the cycloidal drive gearboxes. The design of the wheels consist of an outer tire that is printed from ABS*. A truss design was used in the wheels to aid in the distribution of forces. The tread design prevented the wheels from slipping on the ground and allowed the rover to gain traction. The wheel hub was CNC'd from aluminum 6061 in two parts. The front and back plate were connected by spokes through the wheel. To maintain a constant stiffness surrounding the spokes a stiffness band will be included inside of the wheel.

    Camera Mast

    The camera mast of the rover was also designed The camera mast included a carbon fiber mast with several 3D printed mounts made from PLA attached. These mounts included: lidar, pan tilt camera, ZED cam, GPS, antenna, antenna white box, and the connection mounts to the frame. The lidar mount was attached under the arm mount. The camera mast was attached to the back of the box frame. The goal of the camera mast was to hold all of the rover's needed equipment for CS and other subteams to successfully create redesigns to traverse the URC course.

    Reflection

    Over the course of the semester, I set several personal goals for myself to accomplish as well as teamwide goals for the Drives sub team. These goals included becoming a more effective leader, improve my working knowledge of all sub teams on CMR, and improving my communication skills. Throughout the semester I focused on opportunities to improve my skills and help CMR become a more efficient team.

    One of my proudest accomplishment this semester is taking on the challenge of redesigning all of the systems on Drives. This was an enormous effort to rethink how to improve the past years design, while not having any significant testing data to work with in the analysis. Working through these design iterations took significant time and effort and I am very proud of the improvements made.

    I learned several unexpected things about leadership. One of the main things that I learned was about directing the timeline of a project and understanding how to achieve the project goals with the resources available. It is important to understand the working knowledge of the team in order to better guide and be informed about what challenges might be faced when designing and testing each system.

    Some challenges faced over the course of the semester included the integration of each subsystem of Drives as well as the integration of other subsystems onto the frame of the rover. In order to address this challenge clear communication was necessary between everyone on the team to reach a consensus on the integration design between systems. These issues were addressed, and I assembled the full CAD of the Drives system assembly. A system assembly of the entire rover was also constructed. Potential problems in the future can involve issues in the manufacturing process. In order to mitigate these issues a manufacturing plan will be made.

    During the semester the time I spent doing work for CMR ranged from 14 hours to 25 hours a week. The time spent dedicated to CMR was a lot more than some of my other classes and was difficult at times to manage. Overall, I planned my time well with other courses and completed all of my assignments. I was able to apply knowledge from my courses in product design as well as statics. I also have an interest in Systems Engineering and enjoyed the opportunity to take on a leadership role where I could examine how a full system of many smaller systems is assembled.

    In the future I plan on using my knowledge and skillset to better improve my leadership abilities. I will continue to set multiple deadlines and goals to guide the projects for the eight members of the Drive team. I learned a lot about how to inform and instruct team members that needed guidance as well as plan for the integration of smaller sub systems into the larger design of the rover.
